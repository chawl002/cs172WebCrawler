      <h4>Funding will support Xin Ge's research on antibodies that offer promising therapeutic impact for cancer treatment</h4> 
      <p>Xin Ge, assistant professor of Chemical and Environmental Engineering at UCR's Bourns College of Engineering, has been awarded a five-year Faculty Early Career Development Program (CAREER) grant from the National Science Foundation.<br><br>The $500,000 CAREER Award will support Ge's research on developing therapeutic monoclonal antibodies, which have a broad range of applications in pharmaceutical and biotechnological industries.<br><br>"The annual sale of monoclonal antibodies is more than $75 billion," says Ge. "The market has kept growing rapidly in the last decade and there is no sign of slowdown in the near future."<br><br>Compared with small compound drugs, antibodies usually deliver much better specificity and thus less side effects, Ge explained.<br><br>"Our target is a family of protein cleaving enzymes or proteases, called matrix metalloproteinase, or MMPs in short.<br><br>"MMPs have been recognized as promising therapeutic targets for cancer. Therefore, a great deal of effort has been devoted to generate small molecule inhibitors targeting MMPs. However, all these compound inhibitors failed in clinical trials, due to severe side effects caused by the lack of specificity among the different members of the MMP family."<br><br>In this NSF supported project, Ge aims to develop a panel of novel methods for the discovery of MMP inhibitory antibodies with high potency and high selectivity.<br><br>"We will use rational antibody designs, high-throughput screening, and next-generation sequencing," Ge said. "The combination of these state-of-the-art techniques will make our antibody discovery process faster and more efficiently."<br><br>A graduate of Tsinghua University at Beijing, China, Ge earned his bachelor's degree in Chemical Engineering and his master's degree in Biochemical Engineering. He earned his Ph.D. at McMaster University in Canada, and finished his post-doc training at University of Texas at Austin. He joined the faculty at UCR's Bourns College of Engineering in 2011.<br><br>"I want to thank McMaster and UT Austin professors Carlos Filipe and George Georgiou, who introduced me to this exciting research area and guided me to build the skills and strength to make further contributions," says Ge. "I have been fortunate to join UCR, which provides a friendly and inspiring academic environment to help our young scholars to grow."<br><br>Ge's grant not only helps fund his research project, but also allows him to develop new programs for teaching. NSF CAREER Awards have a built-in educational component that provides support for researchers' efforts to engage and build new generations of scientists.<br><br>"Science is amazing! Doing experiments is a lot of fun! My reason to be a professor and conduct research in a public University is to transfer this joy to the next generation of citizens." Ge adds, "We will continue supporting undergraduate and high school students for their research activities by internships and through our established international collaborations with Chinese universities."<br><br>Link to Ge's lab website:<a href="/gelab/"> http://www.engr.ucr.edu/gelab/</a><br>Link to Ge's CAREER Award on NSF website: <a href="http://www.nsf.gov/awardsearch/showAward?AWD_ID=1453645">http://www.nsf.gov/awardsearch/showAward?AWD_ID=1453645 </a></p>
