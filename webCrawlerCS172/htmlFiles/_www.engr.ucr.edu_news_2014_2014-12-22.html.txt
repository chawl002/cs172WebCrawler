      <h4><em>Software's multi-physics capability supports UCR's multidisciplinary research initiatives</em></h4> 
      <p>Thanks to a generous donation by leading global software company ANSYS, Inc., Bourns College of Engineering (BCOE) students will now be able to use simulation software that enables high-end solving of complex design challenges. The Canonsburg, PA, company's suite of simulation software for product development provides hands-on training in advanced modeling techniques that will enhance students' application of various engineering disciplines.</p> 
      <p>"The worldwide trend for professional engineering teams to become more interdisciplinary means that access to multi-physics solutions such as those ANSYS provides can help better prepare students for the workforce," said Walt Hearn, Regional Director at ANSYS, Inc. "We are delighted to provide that support for training of the next generation of engineers at Bourns College of Engineering, among other leading engineering schools across the U.S. and the world."</p> 
      <p>ANSYS has provided research and teaching licenses of software packages for multi-physics simulations in the areas of electrical, materials and mechanical engineering. The software also includes High Performance Computer (HPC) licenses for cluster computing, so that multiple computers can work together as one supercomputer to solve large-scale problems. BCOE students will use ANSYS software's "solvers" technology to help compute and predict the behavior and effects of structural, thermal, mechanical, computational fluid dynamics, electromagnetics, and other physical forces as students examine such effects on their designs. Comparable to engineering processes in the "real world," student engineers will be able to predict product behavior and performance, test innovative concepts, and optimize designs early in the design and engineering process.</p> 
      <p>Such simulation tools provide significant value in delivering not only the most relevant education to students, but can support the kind of outstanding research that attracts funding, such as cross-university collaborations among faculty and other institutions.</p> 
      <p>"The ANSYS software is a valuable tool to support faculty research and discovery," said Guillermo Aguilar, chair of the department of Mechanical Engineering at BCOE. "Some of our department faculty members are already using a variety of simulation tools for their research. With the new ANSYS licenses in place, I am confident they will be able to better integrate their research projects and coursework into a single very powerful multi-physics platform." In addition to the BCOE researchers and students who have access to the software licenses, Aguilar said the software has the potential to be used across additional disciplines, serving a variety of academic programs and research initiatives throughout the UC Riverside campus.</p> 
      <p>BCOE mechanical engineering professor Hideaki Tsutsui uses ANSYS-CFX to design stem cell bioreactors for scale-up production of human pluripotent stem cells for future clinical applications. ANSYS-CFX allows simulation and optimization of operational parameters without actually manufacturing the full size fluidic devices.</p> 
      <p>Marko Princevac, who specializes in environmental fluid mechanics and played a part in securing the donation, has used the ANSYS-FLUENT software to simulate the airflow around buildings. The software has provided significant value in his work, he said. "Trial and error is something you can't afford in the preparation of long term field measurements," Princevac explained. "Moving instruments during a field study can be inconvenient and prohibitively expensive. Simulations are crucial in determining the most optimal locations and conditions for field deployment."</p> 
      <p>The licenses are held in a secure server and can be checked out to registered users, who can use them on desktop or laptop computers, in classrooms or labs and also via VPN systems. The easy accessibility and multi-user capability provides unprecedented flexibility and learning opportunities using the industry standard.</p> 
      <p>"By using ANSYS software now as part of their student design and product development experiences, BCOE students will be that much more competitive as they enter the industrial workforce," Princevac said. "It also allows our student clubs such as American Society of Mechanical Engineers (ASME) and the Society of Automotive engineers (SAE) to be more competitive in their designs of Human Powered Vehicles (HPV), Formula SAE and Mini Baja challenges," he added, which are challenging projects that involve the design, planning and manufacturing tasks found when introducing a new product to the consumer industrial market.</p>
